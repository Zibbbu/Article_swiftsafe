How well does blockchain technology perform in securing cyber environments?

By Sameer Khan, Published on 15 June 2024

Introduction

Blockchain technology has recently been touted as a universal remedy for all cybersecurity concerns. It offers more robust security for transaction ledgers than traditional centralized computing services. Centralized databases, often targeted by cyberattacks, can bring down entire systems if breached. Blockchain`s use of distributed ledger technology (DLT) eliminates single points of failure. In DLT, compromising one or a few nodes doesn`t impact the rest, ensuring ongoing transparency, availability, and transaction continuity.

However, blockchain cannot always be a panacea, as blockchain applications do not eliminate the need to follow other cybersecurity best practices. In deploying blockchain applications, one must evaluate the governance of the blockchain network, the risks associated with transaction validation, and the possibility of integrity attacks. It's also crucial to assess whether blockchain applications rely on external data or other vulnerable resources.

When designing a blockchain-based cybersecurity solution, it is necessary to examine the vulnerabilities of the end-user environment, the coding and the points at each layer. This concept can be more clearly grasped by analyzing the current situation and considering practical examples that explore the range of solutions offered by blockchain technology.

Understanding DLT

DLT's transparency complicates cyberattackers' efforts to compromise the blockchain with malware or deceptive tactics. A benefit of DLT is its elimination of third-party intermediaries, as each node maintains and circulates a copy of the blockchain. Additionally, DLT addresses endpoint vulnerabilities through layer 2 solutions, enhancing security. Moreover, the security is bolstered by the pre-selection of participants and the establishment of permission layers through cryptographic keys.

DLT technology has advanced, enhancing blockchain`s scalability and speed through its transformation. Innovations like smart contracts and lightning networks are indicative of this progress. Since blockchain`s initial use in cryptocurrency, various forms of DLT have surfaced. Decentralized applications (dApps) that handle financial transactions and power voting and governance systems exemplify the successful application of this technology in real-time scenarios.

Directed acyclic graph (DAG), also known as blockchain 3.0, is another DLT variant that`s gaining traction. Like blockchain, DAGs are peer-to-peer distributed databases with a validation process for collective decision-making. IOTA Tangle and Hedera Hashgraph are examples of the evolving DAG technology. With advancements like DAG in blockchain 3.0, DLT`s vulnerabilities have diminished. DLT maintains consistent ledger copies across nodes, enabling participants such as stakeholders, administrators, and operators to swiftly identify tampering attempts. Blockchain applications use encryption to safeguard individual transactions and the entire ledger, while the consensus mechanism secures new data blocks by necessitating participant validation against previous transactions, reducing the risk of ledger manipulation.

Blockchain in Managing Network Operations

The robustness of blockchain is contingent on its governance structure and transaction validation approach. Different blockchain applications employ varied mechanisms, such as proof of stake, proof of elapsed time, and proof of provenance. For instance, the Linux Foundation`s Hyperledger Fabric project utilizes proof of elapsed time and Byzantine Fault Tolerance methods for these purposes.

Blockchain networks typically use majority consensus for transaction validation, yet they're at risk if a significant number of nodes are hijacked. For instance, public blockchain applications can be compromised by bad actors gaining control of 51 percent of the consensus power, although this is less feasible in large, robust networks. Smaller, private blockchains might be more susceptible, but this risk is often mitigated by vetting participants and implementing user authentication and controls. It`s generally assumed that good actors outnumber bad ones, preventing the latter from dominating a public blockchain. However, the 51 percent consensus could theoretically become a future vulnerability. In private blockchains, while bad actors can cause disruption, they are likely to be identified due to blockchain`s inherent features, albeit potentially after damage is done. Private blockchains tend to be more centralized; for example, Hyperledger requires certification authority approvals for participation.

Another example is project Khoka, where the South African Reserve Bank (SARB) established a financial technology (fintech) task force in 2018 to monitor and promote fintech innovation. A task force was created to review SARB’s position on cryptocurrencies, especially regulatory issues concerning cybersecurity, taxation, consumer protection and anti-money laundering (AML), and to scope out a regulatory sandbox and innovation accelerator. The project used private blockchain among the member banks for intrabank cash transactions.5

Private blockchain could be the answer to these security concerns. Organizations utilizing private blockchain applications should carefully examine the chosen governance models and consensus mechanisms for associated risks. These considerations should be addressed during the design and integration stages by solution architects. Additionally, researchers are focusing on enhancing synchronous hash functions by extending the length of hash outputs.

External Data Dependency

Using the web Practical blockchain applications operate based on real-world data inputs. For instance, a basic financial transaction within a bank or complex operations like supply chain management and international transaction settlements, such as those in ERP systems or interbank global finances, depend on external data involving multiple technologies and transfer modes. These external sources can introduce risks that need to be mitigated by stakeholders. The point is that data traverse through various channels, including blockchain, necessitating comprehensive security measures beyond just blockchain to ensure end-to-end protection.

External data sources fall outside of a blockchain application`s network consensus validation mechanisms. Blockchain networks and stakeholders in the end-to-end transactions must take steps to monitor and ensure data reliability because these elements may be more susceptible to tampering or other malicious actions. Hackers may be able to compromise a project at this layer.

Cybersecurity Weaknesses in Blockchain

In the context of cybersecurity, it`s crucial to recognize that blockchain applications, like any computer system, are susceptible to coding errors that can introduce cyber risks. These errors often arise when network protocols incorporate new features whose vulnerabilities are not yet known.

Blockchain relies on encryption algorithms which, while currently robust, may become compromised as computational methods advance, particularly with the advent of quantum computing. The superior processing capabilities of quantum particles could significantly weaken existing encryption defenses.

Moreover, blockchain applications operate on standard operating systems and platforms that may have known hardware and software vulnerabilities. When implementing blockchain for cybersecurity, organizations should manage these environments as they would any critical business computing resource, adhering to established cybersecurity practices.

Conclusion

Blockchain`s inherent qualities of immutability, transparency, and distributed ledger technology (DLT) offer solutions to contemporary cybersecurity challenges. These attributes aid in safeguarding the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of data. Blockchain is increasingly utilized to mitigate distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks, with transparency ensured by logging each data alteration in the blockchain

While blockchain may be resilient to attacks, the security of the ecosystem it operates within, including the layers and their interconnections, must be considered when designing applications. Relying solely on blockchain`s inherent security features is insufficient; existing security protocols must still be upheld. Coding flaws in blockchain can introduce vulnerabilities, and the inherent risks associated with APIs and their interactions can also present security concerns.
